\begin{abstract}
 This study develops an integrated optimization framework in which policy-inspired carbon scenarios jointly reshape DDMRP strategic buffer positioning and multi-supplier selection and allocation. The modeled environmental boundary covers sourcing, inbound supplier transportation, internal transportation and warehousing emissions. Deterministic lead times and a guaranteed-service setting are considered for a single-product multi-echelon bill of materials.
 The nonlinear formulation is transformed into an explicit mixed-integer linear formulation by linearizing the products between buffer status, decoupled lead time and supplier-selection variables. A numerical evaluation examines scalability, the carbon-price (tax) sweep, emissions caps, the full-factorial combined carbon-price-and-cap design, BOM topology and multi-objective trade-offs, with the carbon price applied per tonne of CO$_2$ after correcting a gram-to-tonne monetization error in the earlier draft. Over the internationally informed price levels the carbon price alone does not displace the cost-optimal decisions, whereas emissions caps reduce emissions at a monotonically increasing compliance cost; the experiments demonstrate deterministic cross-scenario behavior and the DDMRP-based flow-protection intent of the framework, without claiming operational resilience under stochastic disruptions.
 A corrected-unit focused validation shows that all 16 PLM/NLM pairs that both terminate optimally match within solver tolerance. The linearized formulation also solves all 15 tested price, cap and combined stress cases, including BOMs of up to 150 components, to proven optimality in at most 1.58 seconds.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{Intro}
Supply-chain decision makers increasingly need to coordinate service, cost and environmental objectives. This creates a need for decision-support models that expose how sourcing and inventory choices interact with explicitly bounded operational emissions.

The article addresses this strategic decision problem by integrating DDMRP strategic buffer positioning with supplier selection and allocation. It models decision-relevant operational emissions from sourcing, inbound supplier transportation, internal transportation and warehousing, then evaluates carbon-price, emissions-cap and combined price-and-cap scenarios.

Demand Driven Material Requirements Planning emerged as a practitioner-driven evolution of MRP \citep{ptak2019}. Recent optimization and review work automates buffer positioning, parameter tuning or operational DDMRP planning \citep{martin2023,younespour2024,habbadi2025,muyulema2025}, while parallel research on carbon-regulated inventory and green supplier selection and order allocation embeds carbon prices, caps or cap-and-trade rules \citep{absi2013,benjaafar2013,Choi2013,dekker2012,mirzaee2023,hamdan2023,shi2025}. Our earlier studies separately extended strategic positioning for hybrid MTO/MTS systems \citep{achergui1,achergui2}, carbon-aware positioning \citep{achergui3}, and supplier selection \citep{ACHERGUI4}. Section~\ref{sec:lit} reviews these streams in detail. None of them, however, jointly optimizes multi-supplier selection and allocation with explicitly bounded operational carbon emissions under carbon-price, cap and combined price-and-cap scenarios within a guaranteed-service DDMRP strategic positioning model. The present article fills this gap through an integrated framework in which policy-inspired carbon scenarios jointly reshape DDMRP strategic buffer positioning and supplier selection and allocation within an explicitly bounded operational-emissions scope.

The remainder of the article is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec2} defines the DDMRP strategic positioning problem, the multi-supplier allocation setting and the operational carbon-emissions boundary, and Section~\ref{sec:lit} reviews related literature. Section~\ref{sec3} presents the integrated nonlinear formulation and its mixed-integer linearization. Section~4 describes the solution approach. Section~\ref{sec:exp} reports the computational experiments and discusses the results. Section~6 concludes the study.

\section{Problem definition}\label{sec2}
\subsection{DDMRP method}
Demand Driven Material Requirements Planning (DDMRP) is a multi-echelon planning and execution method that blends elements of MRP, Distribution Requirements Planning, lean and the theory of constraints to protect and promote the flow of materials and information \cite{ptak2019}. Its central idea is to decouple the supply chain by placing strategically positioned inventory buffers that absorb demand and supply variability and compress the cumulative lead time seen by the customer. The method is organised around five sequential components: (i) strategic inventory positioning, which decides where buffers should be placed along the bill of materials (BOM); (ii) buffer profiles and levels, which size the buffers; (iii) dynamic adjustments, which adapt buffers to changing conditions; (iv) demand-driven planning, which generates replenishment orders; and (v) visible and collaborative execution.

Each buffer is described by three cumulative zones. The red zone provides safety against variability, the yellow zone covers demand over the decoupled lead time, and the green zone controls the replenishment frequency and order size. These zones are computed from the average daily usage ($adu$), the decoupled lead time of the node, a lead time factor $ltf_{i}$ and a variability factor $varf_{i}$. Replenishment is then governed by the net flow equation, where the net flow position equals on-hand plus on-order inventory minus the qualified demand; an order is launched whenever this position drops into the yellow zone. This work addresses only the first component, strategic inventory positioning, because it conditions the decoupled lead times and therefore the inventory, cost and modeled emissions associated with each buffer.

\subsection{Strategic inventory positioning}
The placement of the decoupling points dictates the decoupled lead time $a_{i}$ of every node of the BOM and, consequently, the average on-hand inventory $aoi_{i}$, the holding cost and the emissions of each potential buffer. Positioning too many buffers inflates the holding cost and the capital immobilization, whereas positioning too few lengthens the lead time exposed to the customer and jeopardizes the service commitment. Strategic inventory positioning therefore consists in selecting the subset of BOM nodes to buffer so that the promised customer service time $SvT$ is honoured at the lowest possible cost.

We adopt the guaranteed service model (GSM) framework \cite{graves2000}, in which every node quotes a guaranteed service time to its successors and is replenished within its decoupled lead time under deterministic assumptions. A buffered node decouples its predecessors and resets lead-time accumulation, while an unbuffered node propagates the lead time of its components. The qualitative DDMRP positioning factors indicate natural buffer candidates; this work casts strategic positioning as an optimization over binary buffer-placement variables $x_i$, coupled with supplier selection and allocation and the modeled operational emissions.

\subsection{DDMRP strategic buffer positioning with multi-supplier allocation and operational carbon emissions}\label{ss:ddmrp}
A deterministic multi-echelon supply-chain environment is considered. A final product $i=1$ and its components $i\in N-\{0,1\}$ are represented by a BOM, while a set of pre-selected eligible suppliers $S$ replenishes raw materials and outsourced parts $i\in Dm$ under a promised service time $SvT$ to customer node $i=0$. The model addresses DDMRP strategic buffer positioning and its inventory implications; dynamic adjustment, demand-driven replenishment and execution are outside its scope. Manufacturing and delivery lead times are deterministic, production and storage capacities are assumed infinite, and supplier capacities are limited.

The models developed in \cite{ACHERGUI4}, coupling buffer positioning with supplier selection, and in \cite{achergui3}, introducing emissions into the positioning problem, provide the basis for jointly accounting for supplier selection and allocation and the operational emissions of the modeled stages. The objective minimizes replenishment and storage costs along the BOM subject to service-time and supplier-capacity constraints. Emissions are incorporated through a price in the objective, a cap constraint, or both simultaneously.
\subsubsection{Carbon emissions modeling}\label{ss:co2}
The emissions function quantifies sources directly affected by buffer positioning and supplier selection and allocation. Within the adopted boundary, $E$ is the sum of internal transportation emissions $TE$, warehousing emissions $WE$, and supplier-side emissions $SE$ for externally delivered materials. This ordering--holding--transportation decomposition follows the structure used in green lot-sizing and SSOA models \citep{lamba2019tfsc,toptal2014}. Other lifecycle stages and Scope 3 categories are outside the model boundary \citep{Matthews2008,huang2009categorization,epa_2022,dekker2012}. Let $X=\{x_i\},\ \forall i\in N$ denote the buffer-positioning decisions.
\begin{equation}
    E(X,Q) = \sum_{\forall i\in N}\sum_{\forall j\in S} E(x_{i},q_{i,j}) = \sum_{\forall i\in N} [TE(x_{i}) + WE(x_{i}) + \sum_{\forall j\in S} SE(q_{i,j})]
\end{equation}
\textbf{Transportation emissions}\\
Getting products from point A to point B is the main line of flow management. Internal between sites or external between echelons of the supply chain, transportation ensures this mission by a multi-modal service provision.
Emissions related to external inventory movements by selected suppliers are included in supplier-side emissions $SE$ detailed later in this section. On a strategic level, this study focuses on internal transportation between the sites that handle the BOM elements. The listing of every replenishment order would be complicated at this level of analysis. For simplification purposes, internal transportation emissions are considered for the average on hand inventory where each item i has a quantity of emissions $\beta_{i}$.

It is observed by the results of the inventory positioning models developed previously that decoupling points allow a reduction of replenishment orders frequency compared to non-decoupled points that had no inventory. This is related to the need for daily deliveries to compensate for the lack of stored parts. The carbon emissions emitted by replenishment activity are then reduced by a factor $R$ for parts with buffers. This factor is related to the dimensions of the buffer, as it covers the maximum lead time of the product's components by which the replenishment is received. Based on previous simulations and the average lead time of the instances studied, for this work, we consider the factor to be equal to $R=3$. The transportation emissions will then be equal to $\beta^{*}=\beta/R$, for the parts where an inventory is positioned. The transportation emissions of an item $i$ are expressed as follows:
\begin{equation}
    TE(x_{i}) = \beta^{*} \cdot aoi_{i} \cdot x_{i} + \beta \cdot aoi_{i} \cdot (1-x_{i}),\quad \forall i\in N\label{eq:TE}
\end{equation}
\textbf{Storing emissions}\\
These emissions occur in the event that a component is stored along the BOM. Setting up an inventory produces two sources of emissions: constant and variable emissions.
\begin{equation}
    WE(x_{i}) = CWE(x_{i}) + VWE(x_{i}), \quad \forall i\in N\label{eq:WE}
\end{equation}
Constant emissions $\alpha^{*}$ are due to fixed operating resources in the storage facility, such as the energy consumed and the human resources needed. It could be product-specific in the case of condition-sensitive products, as they require special storage facilities. For this work, its amount is supposed to be the same for all items, as we consider the same storing conditions.
\begin{equation}
    CWE(x_{i}) = \alpha^{*} \cdot x_{i}, \quad \forall i\in N
\end{equation}
The variable emissions $\alpha_i$ are related to the amount of product stored in the facility and represent handling, deterioration and waste effects within the modeled warehousing boundary.
\begin{equation}
    VWE(x_{i}) = \alpha_{i} \cdot aoi_{i} \cdot x_{i}, \quad \forall i\in N
\end{equation}
\textbf{Supplier-side emissions}\\
Supplier-side emissions are represented by a fixed factor $sup\_Emis$ per allocated unit, including inbound transportation to the unloading dock. This factor allows cleaner suppliers to be selected despite a higher price when required by the active carbon scenario. With $Z=\{z_{i,j}\}$ the supplier-selection variables and $q_{i,j}$ the allocation variables, supplier-side emissions are formulated as follows:
\begin{equation}
    SE(Q) = \sum_{\forall i\in Dm} \sum_{\forall j\in S} z_{i,j} \cdot q_{i,j} \cdot sup\_Emis(j)\label{eq:SE}
\end{equation}
\subsubsection{Carbon reduction strategies}\label{ss:strat}
The modeled emissions are integrated into the strategic buffer-positioning and supplier-selection-and-allocation problem through three policy-inspired decision scenarios: a carbon price, an emissions cap, and a combined carbon-price-and-cap scenario.

\textbf{The carbon tax strategy} monetizes modeled emissions through the parameter $EmisTax$, expressed in constant 2023 EUR per tonne of CO$_2$ (2023 EUR/tCO$_2$), and adds this carbon cost to the objective function \citep{Choi2013,he2015,toptal2014}. Explicit carbon prices are conventionally stated per tonne of CO$_2$ or CO$_2$ equivalent \citep{WorldBankCarbonPricing,OECD2025ECR}; there is no universal international tax rate because rates and coverage differ by jurisdiction. Since this model calculates emissions $E$ in gCO$_2$, the corresponding objective term is $EmisTax\cdot E/10^{6}$. All purchasing, holding and carbon-price inputs must be expressed in constant 2023 EUR.

\textbf{The carbon cap limit strategy} consists in simulating the regulation of emission target caps. Carbon emissions resulting from an activity cycle are limited, and it is up to the company to adapt its operational mode to respect them \citep{Matthews2008,lamba2019cie,chowdhury2020}. It could be by choosing more environmentally friendly but also expensive suppliers or simply investing in better machinery. To keep carbon emissions below this limit $ECap$, a constraint is added to the model that restricts the emission function. This could be considered as an emissions buffer for product's BOM.

\textbf{The combined carbon-price-and-cap scenario} crosses the carbon price with either an explicit no-cap reference or an emissions cap imposed as a hard constraint. It is a policy-inspired decision scenario, not a representation of cap-and-trade, because permits, allowances and trading are not modeled \citep{mirzaee2023,lamba2019cie,rosyidi2024}. Cap-and-trade SSOA and lot-sizing models instead allow permit purchase and sale around a regulated ceiling \citep{he2015,rosyidi2024}. For capped levels, the operating point is shaped jointly by the cap stringency and emissions price.

\subsection{Related literature}\label{sec:lit}
Four research streams frame the present study: DDMRP strategic positioning, carbon-regulated inventory planning, green supplier selection and order allocation (SSOA), and their integration under operational emission boundaries.

\textbf{DDMRP strategic positioning and buffer optimization.}
Beyond practitioner manuals \citep{ptak2019}, empirical assessments confirm DDMRP applicability in complex manufacturing \citep{miclo2019,velasco2020}, while a recent PRISMA-based review shows that strategic inventory positioning, buffer sizing and demand-driven planning remain distinct research themes with limited cross-integration \citep{muyulema2025}. Optimization studies cast buffer placement or parameter setting as mixed-integer programs, metaheuristics or simulation--optimization hybrids \citep{achergui1,achergui2,lahrichi2022,damand2022,martin2023,habbadi2025,xu2023,younespour2024,krajcovic2024}. \citet{favaretto2023} provide a theoretical validation of DDMRP reorder policies through time-varying $(s,S)$ thresholds under service-level constraints. These contributions automate positioning or parameter tuning, and some extend carbon-aware positioning \citep{achergui3} or supplier selection \citep{ACHERGUI4}, but none jointly optimizes multi-supplier allocation with carbon-price, cap and combined price-and-cap scenarios within one guaranteed-service positioning model.

\textbf{Carbon-regulated inventory and lot-sizing.}
Operations-research reviews identify inventory, transportation and sourcing as the main levers through which logistics decisions shape greenhouse-gas outcomes \citep{dekker2012}. Lot-sizing and production-planning models incorporate carbon caps, taxes or cap-and-trade rules at item, period or network level \citep{absi2013,benjaafar2013,Choi2013,lamba2019cie,he2015,toptal2014}. \citet{toptal2014} compare cap, tax and cap-and-trade policies in an EOQ setting with emission-reduction investment, while \citet{he2015} analyze lot-sizing trade-offs under tax and cap-and-trade in a \textit{Journal of Cleaner Production} setting closely aligned with low-carbon manufacturing research. \citet{lamba2019tfsc} and \citet{chowdhury2020} further decompose emissions from ordering, holding and transportation within multi-supplier lot-sizing MINLPs evaluated under alternative regulatory instruments. These studies clarify how regulatory instruments reallocate order quantities and replenishment timing, but they do not represent DDMRP decoupling points along a multi-echelon BOM nor link buffer status to replenishment-frequency effects on transportation emissions.

\textbf{Green supplier selection and order allocation.}
The multi-period, multi-product SSOA problem originates in lot-sizing models that jointly decide order quantities and supplier choice \citep{basnet2005}. Recent SSOA models increasingly combine economic criteria with supplier-specific emission intensities, transport distances or disruption risks \citep{mirzaee2023,hamdan2023,shi2025}. \citet{hamdan2023} formulate a bi-objective integer linear program for dynamic green SSOA with quantity discounts, while \citet{shi2025} optimize procurement cost, quality losses and carbon emissions under disruption risk. \citet{lamba2019cie} compare cap, tax and cap-and-trade regulations within a multi-period SSOA and lot-sizing MINLP, and \citet{rosyidi2024} integrate transport-mode choice with cap-and-trade permit costs in a recent MILP. These models treat supplier choice and order split as primary decisions, but they omit strategic buffer placement on an assembly BOM and the GSM service-time logic that drives decoupled lead times in DDMRP positioning.

\textbf{Integration gap.}
Prior work by the authors separates carbon-aware buffer positioning \citep{achergui3} from supplier-selection coupling \citep{ACHERGUI4}. Recent DDMRP optimization advances positioning, sizing or operational planning \citep{younespour2024,krajcovic2024}, and recent SSOA advances carbon-regulated procurement \citep{hamdan2023,shi2025,rosyidi2024}, yet no published model unifies (i)~binary decoupling-point decisions on a BOM, (ii)~multi-supplier selection and quantity allocation, and (iii)~policy-inspired carbon-price, cap and combined price-and-cap scenarios within one explicit mixed-integer framework bounded to sourcing, inbound supplier transportation, internal transportation and warehousing emissions. The combined scenario differs from cap-and-trade SSOA models \citep{mirzaee2023,rosyidi2024} by fixing permit markets outside the decision scope. The formulation developed in Section~\ref{sec3} addresses this gap.

The preceding subsections define the emissions boundary, the three carbon scenarios and their link to buffer placement and supplier decisions. Section~\ref{sec3} integrates these elements into a single strategic optimization model.
\section{Problem formulation}\label{sec3}
The integrated strategic buffer-positioning and supplier-selection-and-allocation problem is first expressed as a nonlinear mixed-integer formulation. The model accounts for decision-relevant operational carbon emissions within the sourcing, inbound transportation, internal transportation and warehousing boundary. An explicit linearization is then introduced to improve numerical tractability.

The developed model answers the following three questions at once:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Select eligible suppliers and allocate the required quantities of raw materials and outsourced parts among them.
    \item Position inventories along the components of the BOM, in a combination where it does not cost much and does synchronize with supply capacities.
    \item Depending on the chosen strategy, the objective would be to reduce the amount of emissions emitted by different activities, and hence the cost of the tax penalties. Or it would be limiting the amount of CO2 emissions and obliging the model to choose more ecological alternatives.
\end{itemize}

The model outputs the buffer positions, supplier selections and allocations, and the decision-relevant operational emissions $E$. Its final expression is formulated using Equation \ref{eq:TE}, Equation \ref{eq:WE} and Equation \ref{eq:SE} as follows:
\begin{equation}
     E(X,Q) = \sum_{\forall i\in N} [[\alpha^{*} + (\alpha_{i}+ \beta_{i}^{*}-\beta_{i})\cdot aoi_{i}]\cdot x_{i} + \beta_{i}\cdot aoi_{i} + \sum_{\forall j\in S} q_{i,j}\cdot sup\_Emis_{j}]\label{eq:emissions}
\end{equation}
For the \textbf{Carbon emissions Tax} strategy, the emissions compensation cost is added to the objective function. It would be minimized in parallel to storing costs and purchasing costs of raw materials. The model in this case is formulated as follows:

\begin{gather}
    \min TC = RawMCost + \sum_{\forall i\in N} StC_{i} + EmisTax \cdot \frac{E(X,Q)}{10^{6}}\\
    \nonumber S.T.\\
    \nonumber SvT_{0} \leq SvT\\
    \nonumber a_{i} = t_{i} + \max_{k\in comp_{i}}[(1-x_{k})\cdot a_{k}],\quad \forall i \in N\setminus Dm \\
    \nonumber a_{d} = t_{d} + \max_{j\in SL_{d}} z_{d,j}\cdot sup\_Deliv_{d,j},\quad \forall d \in Dm
\end{gather}

\textbf{The Carbon cap limit} strategy holds the total emissions at the limit of $ECap$ which is added as a second hard constraint to the model in addition to the service time. This limit is considered a reasonable cap to allow some maneuverability in managing the different operations. The model for this strategy is expressed as follows:

\begin{gather}
    \min TC = RawMCost + \sum_{\forall i\in N} StC_{i}\\
    \nonumber S.T.\\
    \nonumber SvT_{0} \leq SvT\\
    \nonumber E(X,Q) \leq ECap\\
    \nonumber a_{i} = t_{i} + \max_{k\in comp_{i}}[(1-x_{k})\cdot a_{k}],\quad \forall i \in N\setminus Dm \\
    \nonumber a_{d} = t_{d} + \max_{j\in SL_{d}} z_{d,j}\cdot sup\_Deliv_{d,j},\quad \forall d \in Dm
\end{gather}
\section{Solution approach}
This section presents the solution approach for the integrated DDMRP strategic buffer-positioning and supplier-selection-and-allocation problem. The original formulation contains products between binary buffer or supplier decisions and integer decoupled lead times. Following \cite{achergui2}, these products are replaced by explicit linearization variables, yielding a mixed-integer linear formulation solved with CPLEX.

The big-$M$ method replaces $a_i x_i$ with $y_i$ and $y_i z_{i,j}$ with $v_{i,j}$. The bound $M$ is derived from the sum of processing times and the maximum eligible supplier lead time. Supplier-selection variables are linked to strictly positive allocations and supplier eligibility. Two binary scenario indicators control which carbon mechanism is active: $\tau$ activates the carbon-price term in the objective function, and $\kappa$ activates the emissions-cap constraint. The three scenarios are recovered as special cases of a unified model:
\begin{gather}
    (\tau,\kappa)=
    \begin{cases}
      (1,0), & \text{Tax strategy}\\
      (0,1), & \text{Cap limit strategy}\\
      (1,1), & \text{Hybrid strategy}
    \end{cases}
\end{gather}
Let $H_i=adu\cdot aihc_i\cdot(1.5+varf_i)\cdot ltf_i\cdot up_i\cdot rqtf_i$. Using Equation \ref{eq:emissions}, the linearized model is written compactly as follows:
\begin{gather}
    \min \, TC = RawMCost(Q)+\sum_{\forall i\in N}H_i\left[y_i+\sum_{\forall j\in S}su_{i,j}sup\_price_jv_{i,j}\right]+\tau EmisTax\frac{E(X,A,Y,Q)}{10^{6}}\\
\nonumber \textbf{S.t.}\\
    SvT_{0} \leq SvT\\
    \kappa \cdot E(X,Q) \leq ECap\\
    0 \leq a_i \leq M,\quad \forall i \in N\\
    0 \leq y_{i} \leq a_i,\quad \forall i \in N\\
    y_{i} \leq M \cdot x_{i},\quad \forall i \in N\\
    y_{i} \geq a_{i}- M \cdot (1-x_{i}),\quad \forall i \in N\\
    0 \leq v_{i,j}\leq y_i,\quad \forall i\in N,\forall j\in S\\
    v_{i,j}\leq Mz_{i,j},\quad \forall i\in N,\forall j\in S\\
    v_{i,j}\geq y_i-M(1-z_{i,j}),\quad \forall i\in N,\forall j\in S\\
    z_{d,j}\leq q_{d,j}\leq z_{d,j}su_{d,j}sup\_cap_{d,j},\quad \forall d\in Dm,\forall j\in S\\
    z_{d,j}\leq su_{d,j},\quad \forall d\in Dm,\forall j\in S\\
    \sum_{\forall j \in S}q_{d,j} = adu \cdot rqtf_{d},\quad \forall d\in Dm\\
    a_{i} \geq t_{i} + (1-x_{k}) \cdot a_{k}, \quad \forall i \in N\setminus Dm, \quad \forall k \in comp_{i}\\
    a_{d} \geq t_{d} + z_{d,j}\cdot sup\_deliv_{d,j}, \quad \forall d \in Dm
\end{gather}
We maintain throughout this work the same resolution strategy as used in our previous models \cite{achergui2,ACHERGUI4}. The proposed model is solved in parallel with its non-linear version. This solution approach allows a benchmark of the results obtained by the pseudo-linearization. The behavior of the model around different types of BOMs is also a key data for future perspectives of this research work.

\section{Experimentation}\label{sec:exp}
The numerical evaluation covers computational scalability, carbon-policy scenarios, service-time sensitivity, multi-level and parallel BOM topologies, multi-objective Pareto analysis, near-optimal decision-degeneracy diagnostics, and a comparison of the linearized formulation with its nonlinear counterpart. Following a unit audit, the carbon price is applied as $EmisTax$ in currency per tonne of CO$_2$, with model emissions expressed in grams and converted to tonnes by dividing by $10^{6}$ before the price enters the cost objective. The earlier per-gram interpretation, which inflated every tested carbon price by a factor of $10^{6}$, has been corrected, and all experiment families---scalability, the carbon-price (tax) sweep, the cap sweep, the full-factorial combined carbon-price-and-cap design, service-time sensitivity, topology sensitivity, Pareto analysis, the near-optimal decision-degeneracy diagnostics and the PLM/NLM validation---are reported on this corrected basis. CPLEX 20.1.0 solves the linearized formulation and CP Optimizer solves the nonlinear formulation on a machine with an Intel i7-12700K 3.61 GHz and 32GB of RAM running Windows 10. A 300-second limit is imposed on every run. Solver termination status and optimality gaps are retained so that proven-optimal solutions are distinguished from time-limited incumbents. All runs remain reported, but behavioral comparisons admit only proven-optimal solutions and time-limited feasible incumbents with a final optimality gap no greater than $1\%$; larger-gap incumbents are excluded from comparative conclusions. For representative proven-optimal scenarios, additional near-optimal probes maximize buffer-position, supplier-selection and allocation deviations while constraining the original objective to remain within $1\%$ of the optimum; these probes diagnose whether a small objective gap hides materially different decisions without multiplying the full policy sweep. The resulting Jaccard similarities and allocation deviations are reported as continuous diagnostics rather than stable/unstable classes, because no industrial tolerance threshold is imposed. They are not interpreted as stochastic robustness tests, which would require demand variability, supplier disruption, or execution-dynamics experiments. Throughout this section, emissions are reported in tonnes of CO$_2$ ($1\,\text{t}=10^{6}\,$gCO$_2$).
\subsection{Benchmark instances and simulation scenarios}
The supply chain network environment considered previously is kept to ensure the accuracy of the generated instances. The benchmark set spans BOM sizes from $N=2$ to $N=150$ components and includes both tree-structured assemblies and dedicated multi-level and parallel-branch topologies. In this section, simulation scenarios denote controlled deterministic optimization scenarios, not Monte Carlo, stochastic demand, disruption, or dynamic execution simulations. They test the three carbon-policy settings for the BOMs considered. The corrected carbon-price comparison uses a no-price baseline and $EmisTax\in\{15,50,75,100\}$ constant 2023 EUR/tCO$_2$. These internationally informed levels span a low observed carbon-tax signal and progressively stronger policy scenarios, but they are study-defined comparison values rather than statutory rates or a direct reproduction of a single international benchmark \citep{OECD2025ECR,HighLevelCommission2017}. The emissions baseline is selected with CPLEX's native lexicographic objective, $staticLex(TotalCostCS, Emis)$: economic cost is minimized first with no carbon price and no cap, and supplier-side emissions are then minimized as the internal tie-break objective. The emission cap $ECap$ is tightened progressively from $100\%$ down to $70\%$ of this reproducible baseline.
The same suppliers details seen previously are considered with the same adaptation for their capacities to accommodate the higher demand of complex BOMs. A supplier-side emissions quantity per product is added to these details as discussed in the previous section. Their details are updated and presented in \cref{tab:suppdet2}.

We assume that internal trips are made on the factory premises using the same vehicle. For an average travel distance, we fix the amount of transportation emissions generated in a whole activity cycle to $\beta = 1500 gCO2/unit$. Supplier-side emissions include the transportation emissions of the selected suppliers until the unloading dock. These emissions are not correlated with the price tag of the suppliers contrary to their delivery time, which is correlated. For simplification purposes, the pre-selected suppliers $S$ are the same for all the components of the BOMs, and all supplier related detail for a non-delivery material is null.

\subsection{Results and analysis}
The corrected analysis reports every experiment family on the per-tonne carbon-price basis. The key outputs are the actual service time $SvT_{0}$, total cost $TC$, decision-relevant operational carbon emissions $E$, number of positioned buffers, days of inventory outstanding (DIO, computed as the sum of decoupled lead times), computational time $RT$, solver status and optimality gap. All runs are reported; behavioral comparisons draw only on comparison-admissible solutions, that is proven-optimal solutions and time-limited feasible incumbents whose final optimality gap does not exceed $1\%$.

\subsubsection{Computational scalability}
Table~\ref{tab:scal} reports the behavior of the pseudo-linear model on a baseline scenario (no carbon policy) for BOM sizes ranging from $N=2$ to $N=150$ components. All instances are solved to optimality, and the solving time stays on the order of a second across the whole range: most instances solve in well under one second, the largest ($N=150$) in under one second, and the slowest cases ($N=35$ and $N=123$) in about $5.5$ seconds. This confirms that the pseudo-linearization keeps the integrated positioning and supplier-selection problem tractable at an industrial scale, well within the time windows required for strategic and tactical planning. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:scalrt}, the runtime does not grow monotonically with $N$: it is driven by the combinatorial structure of each BOM (its depth, branching and the resulting supplier-selection sub-problem) rather than by the node count alone.

The number of positioned buffers and the resulting DIO grow with BOM complexity (Figure~\ref{fig:scalbuf}), from a single buffer for the smallest chains up to 57 buffers for the largest assembly. Here again the relation is not strictly monotonic in $N$: instances with a flatter or more parallel structure concentrate the decoupling on very few nodes, whereas deeper assemblies require more buffers to protect the promised service time. The baseline emissions reported in Table~\ref{tab:scal} and visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:scalemis} follow the same structural dependence; they are governed by the quantities flowing through the BOM and by the selected suppliers, so they scale with complexity while varying between instances of comparable size.

\subsubsection{Carbon tax strategy}
Under the carbon tax strategy, monetized emissions are added to the cost objective, so the model trades off purchasing and holding costs against the emission penalty $EmisTax\cdot E$, with $E$ expressed in tonnes. Table~\ref{tab:tax} reports the total emissions of the six representative instances across the corrected carbon-price comparison levels $EmisTax\in\{0,15,50,75,100\}$ constant 2023 EUR/tCO$_2$. Over this range the emissions---and the underlying buffer positions and supplier allocations---are invariant: for every tested instance the highest price yields exactly the baseline emissions, a $0\%$ reduction. At these internationally informed price levels the monetized carbon term stays small relative to the procurement- and inventory-cost differences between alternative sourcing and buffering decisions, so it does not displace the cost-optimal solution. This is the substantive correction relative to the earlier per-gram draft, in which the price was overstated by a factor of $10^{6}$ and therefore appeared to reshape the decisions: once the price is applied per tonne, a hard emissions cap, rather than the price, is what drives the emission response over the tested range.

\subsubsection{Carbon cap strategy}
Under the cap strategy, a hard constraint forces the total emissions below a ceiling $ECap$, expressed as a percentage of the baseline emissions. Table~\ref{tab:cap} reports the total cost as the cap is tightened from $100\%$ down to $70\%$ of the baseline, and Figure~\ref{fig:cap} shows the resulting compliance-cost curves. As expected, the cost increases monotonically with the stringency of the cap, since the model is forced to adopt cleaner but more expensive suppliers and to reposition buffers to meet the emission target. The compliance cost is markedly non-linear: it remains modest for caps down to about $85\%$ of the baseline and rises sharply for the tightest caps. Over the tested instances, the cost premium at the $70\%$ cap ranges from roughly $8\%$ to $46\%$, the steepest curves corresponding to the instances whose baseline solution is already close to their cleanest feasible configuration.

\subsubsection{Hybrid strategy}
The combined-scenario experiment crosses $EmisTax$ with either no cap or a hard emissions cap. The corrected experiment uses a full factorial design, testing every carbon-price comparison level against an explicit no-cap level and hard ceilings from $100\%$ to $70\%$ of baseline emissions. The $100\%$ level remains a hard baseline-emissions ceiling and is distinct from no cap. Infeasible combinations are retained and reported rather than omitted or relaxed, because they identify the achievable emissions frontier; in the present campaign all $160$ tested combinations are feasible and solved to proven optimality. Consistent with the carbon-price result, the achieved emissions are invariant to $EmisTax$ at every cap level---the cap alone governs them, decreasing monotonically as the ceiling tightens from the baseline down to $70\%$---while the price leaves the cost-optimal buffer positions and supplier allocations unchanged. This design distinguishes unconstrained price-only behavior, compliance at the baseline ceiling, and increasingly stringent cap effects; the complete per-combination results are tabulated in Appendix~\ref{app:results} (Table~\ref{tab:hybridfull}).

\subsubsection{Near-optimal decision-degeneracy diagnostic}
For representative proven-optimal policy anchors, extremal alternative solutions are computed that maximize the deviation in buffer positions, supplier selections and allocation quantities while constraining the original objective to remain within $1\%$ of the optimum. Table~\ref{tab:stability} reports the resulting continuous similarity and deviation metrics---the minimum buffer-position and supplier-selection Jaccard similarities and the maximum normalized allocation $L_{1}$ deviation---for each anchor; no binary stable/unstable label is assigned and no industrial tolerance threshold is imposed. Across the anchors the buffer positions remain comparatively similar (buffer Jaccard frequently above $0.5$ and reaching $1$), whereas the supplier selections and allocation quantities vary substantially (supplier Jaccard as low as about $0.18$, and normalized allocation deviation exceeding $1$ for the smallest instances). The diagnostic therefore indicates that the cost-optimal objective value is attainable through materially different sourcing and allocation decisions while the decoupling structure stays comparatively concentrated. These are continuous diagnostics of decision degeneracy run on representative anchors only; they are not interpreted as stochastic robustness tests, which would require demand variability, supplier-disruption or execution-dynamics experiments.

\subsubsection{Pseudo-linear versus non-linear model}
Finally, the corrected-unit linearized formulation (PLM) is benchmarked against the nonlinear formulation (NLM) solved with CP Optimizer under a common $60$-second validation limit. Across small instances and all three carbon scenarios, 16 pairs terminate optimally in both formulations and all match within solver tolerance. Five additional NLM runs return time-limited feasible incumbents equal to the PLM optimum but cannot prove optimality within the limit. All 15 larger PLM stress cases up to 150 components terminate optimally in at most $1.58$ seconds. These results support empirical validation of the linearized formulation over the tested domain, rather than a claim of universal mathematical equivalence.

\section{Conclusion} \label{vrptwsynexact:sec:end}
%=====================================================================
This article proposes an integrated sustainable DDMRP decision framework in which policy-inspired carbon-price, emissions-cap and combined price-and-cap scenarios jointly reshape strategic buffer positioning and supplier selection and allocation. Relative to recent DDMRP optimization studies that automate buffer placement or integrate strategic and operational planning \citep{martin2023,younespour2024,habbadi2025,krajcovic2024}, to carbon-regulated lot-sizing and inventory models \citep{he2015,toptal2014,chowdhury2020}, and to green supplier-selection models that optimize order allocation under carbon regulation \citep{mirzaee2023,hamdan2023,shi2025,lamba2019cie,rosyidi2024}, the contribution lies in unifying these decisions within one guaranteed-service positioning model and an explicitly bounded operational-emissions function. The model minimizes cost while satisfying the promised deterministic service-time threshold and the active carbon-scenario conditions. Its environmental conclusions are limited to the modeled boundary of sourcing, inbound supplier transportation, internal transportation and warehousing.

The carbon-price scenario monetizes emissions in the objective, the cap scenario enforces an emissions ceiling, and the combined carbon-price-and-cap scenario activates both mechanisms. These are policy-inspired decision scenarios rather than faithful simulations of particular regulatory systems.

The nonlinear formulation is transformed into an explicit mixed-integer linear formulation by linearizing the buffer--lead-time and supplier--buffer interactions, enforcing supplier eligibility, and linking selection to positive allocated quantities. The focused validation demonstrates matching objectives within solver tolerance for all jointly optimal PLM/NLM test pairs, while the PLM proves optimality rapidly for every tested stress case up to 150 components. The study therefore supports an empirically validated linearized formulation and deterministic cross-scenario robustness, not universal equivalence or stochastic resilience.

Future work should test the resulting system against demand variability, supplier disruptions and execution dynamics to evaluate whether its DDMRP-based flow-protection intent translates into operational resilience. Additional extensions include richer supplier quality and reliability attributes, jointly optimizing the emissions price and cap stringency, and validating the framework with industrial data.
